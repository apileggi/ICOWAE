Study Overview:

Thank you for taking the time to participate in our study! We are incredibly grateful for your help.

 

We are interested in studying a type of associative learning that involves learning contingencies (MacLeod, 2019). Very simply, when two events are experienced together and thereafter co-occur with a relatively high frequency, we learn to associate those two events with one another. Research using the color-word contingency paradigm has found that reaction times are speeded for highly associated events whereas reaction times are slowed for events that co-occur less often (Lin & MacLeod, 2018) and the difference in reaction times is what we refer to as the contingency learning effect. Interestingly, this effect is reliable even when participants are unaware that they are responding to high/low association pairings.

In the first task of our study, we used the color-word contingency paradigm so that you would learn to associate specific color-word pairs and demonstrate the contingency learning effect through your response times. In the second task, we asked you to categorize words as either English words or non-words. We were looking to see if the difference in response times for previously learned color-word contingencies would persist and transfer to a situation where the response task is no longer related to color identification. Some research using a different paradigm has shown that learned associations can transfer to new contexts (Schmidt & De Houwer, 2012) and so we hypothesize that we too will find evidence of the transfer of learning to new response contexts. What makes our study different from the previous research is that we are interested in investigating the transfer of associations that were specifically learned by the color-word contingency paradigm.
